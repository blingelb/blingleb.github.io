Introduction

        

        Imagine it's the starting weeks of the Fall Semester at Purdue.  You and your new friends have just wrapped up an outdoor game of Spikeball over by the Intramural Fields and are looking for something to do.  All of a sudden, you see a Starship roll past, nothing out of the ordinary on Purdue’s campus, but this one looks different.  There’s a plate with what looks like a projector behind it, and arms that appear to fold out.  You check the Starship app and notice there’s a new feature, Movees.

        Out of curiosity, you check out this new service.  The app prompts for a location to deliver a Starship, and you request for it to arrive as soon as possible.  After a few minutes, the Starship arrives, unfolds its screen, and displays a prompt for you to stream your phone to the Starship.  Now all of a sudden, you’ve started an outdoor movie night, with new friends coming and going watching the latest movies and TV shows, all while enjoying the wonderful late-summer evening.  

        This is just one of many experiences that could be brought from this idea. Movees creates human-to-human experiences in an outdoor setting from a service that once eliminated these types of interactions.  With this project, we hope to change the once solitary mental model that Starship once had to be more of an opportunity to meet new friends, create new memories, and have a fun evening outdoors.

Step 1: Ideation

The first step of project 1 was utilizing prior knowledge about starships and delivery systems to identify possible solutions to the problem:

“How to utilize the 60 starships to provide users with a human-to-human (H2H) experience that also changes the mindset regarding starships?”

 

In our initial brainstorm session, we came up with the following ideas:

Book Delivery / Entertainment Exchange - This idea involves mobile libraries in which Starships deliver books to students in exchange of a deposited book from that student, similar to public library booths found in parks.

Donations & Give Away - At the end of the semester, leftover items such as school supplies, dorm supplies, food, etc. can be collected by the Starship and delivered to appropriate outlets for donations.

Lend Tools - Starships would be equipped with tools in order to assist students with any needs they require.  This includes tools for fixing bikes, making repairs in dorm rooms, or even sewing kits to mend clothing.

Games - Various games were thought up as a part of the ideation process.  Mostly, these games involved seeking out the Starships based on location and teamplay to encourage human-to-human collaboration.  

Conclusion: The ideation process was the first step into the design process and posed the first test for our group’s ability to conceptualize potential solutions for the project.  Originally, our group was dead set on the Book Delivery idea, which was created before the interview process.  However, after feedback from both our interviewees and our peers, we had to backpedal to a new idea.

This experience taught our group that in order to change the mental model of the Starship, we had to first look beyond the idea of delivery, as a majority of our ideas were focused on that.  From one of our interviews, the idea of a ‘mobile movie theater’ came up, an idea that would eventually become our final design.  However, we had a ways to go before this idea could be fully realized, as will be discussed further into the design process.

Group Takeaways

Starship Questions

Example Questions: 

Have you used a starship before? Why choose starship over other alternatives? Would you use starship to deliver other items?

Key Takeaways:

From starship questions, we aimed to understand what the student usage of starship looks like. We learned that not a lot of people use starships. Many stated that they only use starship occasionally and don’t know many others who use it actively. The most common reason they stated was that starships are inconvenient and slow. Alternatives such as Doordash or getting food themselves were deemed more convenient by most.

Reading Questions

Example Questions: 

Do you read books? How often do you read? Have you used a public library before? Would you read more if starship allowed book exchanges?

Key Takeaways:

From reading questions, we hoped to gauge the practicality and interest of people regarding our book exchange idea. We learned that reading has become drastically uncommon, as many stated reading most during their primary education and during their adolescence. Additionally, most entertainment and leisure activities (including reading) are stated to be done digitally compared to their physical counterparts. We concluded that people aren’t interested in reading and book exchanges would not incentivize people enough to read.

Games/Hobbies/Ideas Questions

Example Questions: 

How often do you play board games? Would you be interested in campus-wide games? What type of activities do you participate in?

Key Takeaways:

From the ideas questions, we requested ideas and feedback from our interviewees regarding other ideas involving starships and how they felt about them. We knew beforehand that people are most active recreationally in the evening to night, especially Friday nights. We learned that people might consider participating in campus-wide games if it didn’t interfere with their day schedule. Ideas that physically interacted with the starship, like slapping, or kicking it, showed some potential. Most importantly, many said that they spend their free time in group leisure activities with friends after classes in the evening.

Step 3: Post-Interview Evaluation

After evaluating interviews, we decided to revise our idea and think of something beside book exchange. We learned from our interviews, professor, and initial problem that there are three main points our idea should hit on: 1) human-to-human interaction (H2H), 2) reuse starship capabilities (why starship?), and 3) change the mindset regarding starships.

Final Idea

We decided on a “starship projector” idea that has a projector attached to it (a special starship robot) with small but powerful speakers inside the starship. Users would request a starship using the app at any location within campus radius. They can then watch a movie using the starship on any wall or an easy to set up projector screen that comes with the starship. We noticed that this solution hits all the aforementioned criteria:

  1. Human-To-Human
  1. Friends can have movie nights outside (most of the year is nice) and bond.
  2. Random people are more inclined to join in (thus making new friends).
  1. Reuse Starship
  1. Can set up movie nights anywhere (any wall or field), utilizing starship mobility.
  2. Can request any time (when dark of course), utilizing starship infrastructure.
  1. Change Mindset
  1. People see starships as outdoor movie experiences.
  2. Starship is not just delivery robots anymore.

Issues

As with any initial solution however, there are details, edge cases, and issues that need to be worked out. Below we detail the solution to each issue we thought of.

Issue: How can people choose movies?

Solution:

        Streaming, HDMI, or Screen Sharing

Issue: Not nice walls nearby or want to watch in IM Fields?

Solution:

Provide easy-setup projector screens that may or may not be attached to the starship itself.

Issue: People streaming inappropriate content for movies?

Solution:

Special UI hooks that allows users to connect streaming platform accounts to the app (and thus projector), and moderates streaming content appropriate to play. OR

Projector comes with a pre-curated catalog of options to select from. OR
Website blocking and streaming moderation software.

Issue: People want to join but don’t know where movies are played?

Solution:

App sets an event point or marker on a GPS map that other app users can see.

Issue: More incentive for people to join?

Solution:

Ordering food from starships which destination located at events may receive special, limited discounts.

Revise

The resulting questions are a result of questions that we received as part of feedback or came to us as a result of presenting our idea. We formatted this similar to the “Issues” section in the earlier step.

Problem: How would they invite their friends to join?

Solution:

There will be a map app associated with starship-movies. As long as the host requests a movie robot, the spot he requested will appear on the map and people could see and join the movie. Also, the host can get an URL link and share it on any social media to invite people.

Problem: How would they decide and select a movie?

Solution:

Mainly the choice of movie is on the host. Before the robot comes, the host can negotiate with people about the choice. Once the robot has arrived, the host will select and play the movie in following ways: Login to their video account on the robot and play. To prevent people from joining a movie that they aren't interested in, we will post the title of the movie on the app.

Problem: How would they connect their devices?

Solution:

For security purposes and content filtering, while also considering that people may not even have a device on hand, we decided to remove the ability to connect from devices, and instead allow them to connect their streaming accounts or pre-curated solution.

Problem: How would they be controlling sound, brightness etc?

Solution:

There will be restrictions on the sound and brightness at all places. For the area that we think that wouldn’t disturb people, we call it the happy zone, the maximum volume and brightness will be higher. Also, the users could send requests for making some area to be a happy zone, the request will be reviewed by our staff.

Problem: How would they know about the movie screening, whether there is enough space to sit and watch?

Solution:

As mentioned, we will have a map app or work with some map app to let people know where the movie will be. Just like traffic analysis in map apps, we gather people’s location information on the map and analyze the number of people who are watching the movie. If there's not enough space, we will set a warning on the map.

Problem: How would you address interruptions during the movie, like weather changes?

Solution:

If facing weather changes or any other situations that make the movie can’t continue, the starship robot will give an announcement 10 minutes before leaving. After 10 minutes, the robot will close the screen and leave. The host will get a refund and some coupons as compensation.

Problem: How do we protect our robot and projector from being damaged?

Solution:

First of all, to protect our robot and projector from damage, we encase the projector lens within a hinged shell, allowing it to remain shielded while the robot is in motion, thus preventing any potential collisions. We didn’t set any protection during the projection, instead, we will restrict the location to prevent the robot giving a movie at some crowded main passway area, to protect the robot from damage by accident. We didn’t set any protection for intentional damage by the user because we believe Purdue students are kind and demonstrate a high level of integrity.

Problem: How do we prevent people from projecting movies in inappropriate areas? (eg. academic buildings, main road).

Solution:

There will be a restriction that people can only do movies in some areas. However, when people plan to do a movie night that is not in the allowed area, they can send a request and our staff will check whether the location is appropriate. If the location was approved, it will be added into the map and no more requests needed for future users to do projection on that area.

Step 5: Final Features

These are the features that we want to add into our project to solve the problems in the midpoint evaluation.

App-We will have a map app or collaborate with some map app to show the locations that has movie presenting. The user will use the app to send requests for a movie night, streaming movies through their video account (Netflix, Hulu, etc) and manage location requests and payment. Meanwhile, if there’s some issue like construction or road close, the map will also update to make an existing green area to be red. As a participant, users could check on the coming movie events. The app will gather the GPS data from users’ phones to estimate the people attending the movie event. If it’s beyond the recommended capacity of the area, a warning will be shown on the map.

Map-In the map feather in our app there will be some predetermined areas that can allow people to do movies. In the app, those areas will be colored as green or blue. The difference between blue and green is that the blue area is called the “happy zone” which allows people to project movies with fewer restrictions on brightness and volume. People can also project movies in red areas but only after being approved by our staff. After the approval, the area will become green for future movies.

Protection-To safeguard our robot and projector against potential damage, we've implemented a protective measure by enclosing the projector lens within a hinged shell. This arrangement ensures that the lens remains shielded while the robot is in motion, minimizing the risk of collisions. Additionally, we've opted not to implement protection measures during projection. Instead, we've chosen to restrict the robot's location to avoid projecting in crowded areas, thus reducing the likelihood of accidental damage. Furthermore, we've decided against implementing safeguards against intentional damage by users, as we trust in the integrity of Purdue students, believing that they will treat the equipment with care and respect.

Screen-There will be a foldable screen installed inside the robot. Once the projection is happening, the robot will eject the arm and unfold the screen. We are doing this to make it convenient for people to use our robot because no one would like to spend time installing and uninstalling the screen kit, and projecting movies directly on a random wall is of low quality.

Appendix A - Sketches

Sketching is a cheap yet effective way to create the first visual examples of our final product.  Sketching is an essential part of design as it allows the designer to visually represent their ideas without needing to commit to a final design.  As seen by the sketches below, each member contributed a wide array of ideas that may or may not make it to the final product. However, each sketch hosts new ideas that contribute to the final design, no matter if all of its components make it to the final product or not.  

Starship Design - These figures demonstrate the physical appearance of the Starship itself, including projectors, speakers, and inputs.  Labeling each of the components is important to dictate to the user what is being represented in the sketch.

Projector Setup - One of the tricker design decisions for the project was how to display the projector.  Originally, we wanted to project onto any wall on campus.  However, we realized that this idea would limit the number of locations where Starship Movies could go.  Instead, we opted to design a screen for the projector, including a variety of systems that would allow for the screen to prop up on its own with minimal user intervention.
